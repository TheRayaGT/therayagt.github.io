I believe every Mathematician has his/her favourite formula. I do have one as well and of course, it belongs to the realm of Number Theory. That is

\( p(n) \sim \dfrac{1}{4n\sqrt{3}}\operatorname{exp}\left(\pi\sqrt{\dfrac{2n}{3}}\right) \) for large \(n\).

This formula for the number of partitions \(p(n)\) of an integer was developed by G. H. Hardy and Srinivasa Ramanujan. In my opinion, it is so beautiful since it reminds me of the most beautiful mathematical equation coined by Leonhard Euler which is \(e^{i\pi}+1=0\). The asymptotic formula for the number of partitions of partitions encompasses both \(\pi\) and \(e\), which are two of the most (actually 'the two most') famous constants in Mathematics.

Research

I am particularly interested in problems in Real and Complex Analysis, Algebraic and Analytic Number Theory and Linear Algebra. Currently, I am investigating some interesting topics in Analytic Number Theory which include the prime number theorem, prime gaps and sieve methods.

Here are some of the problems which I have worked on.

ANALYTIC NUMBER THEORETIC METHODS IN SOLVING A DIOPHANTINE EQUATION (2022)

Find all solutions to the Diophantine Equation \(\displaystyle\prod_{k=1}^{n}\left(k^2+1\right)=b^2\).

This has some relation to some major results in Number Theory. These include the law of quadratic reciprocity, Fermat's Sum of Two Squares Theorem, the prime number theorem, Bertrands's Postulate and some bounds involving the prime-counting function \(\pi(n)\), as well as Chebyshev's First and Second Functions, denoted by \(\vartheta(n)\) and \(\psi(n)\) respectively.
